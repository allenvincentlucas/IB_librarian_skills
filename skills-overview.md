<span class="eyebrow">CORE FRAMEWORK</span>

Stepping into an International Baccalaureate (IB) secondary library is an exciting shift. Unlike traditional libraries where the focus might lean heavily on quiet study and circulation management, an IB secondary library functions as the collaborative hub of the school's academic ecosystem.
To thrive across the Middle Years Programme (MYP) and Diploma Programme (DP), a new IB librarian needs to master a distinct set of framework-specific skills and learn where to find the best curricular resources.
## 1. Core Framework Knowledge & Skills
To support students and teachers effectively, you have to speak the language of the IB. Understanding how the library explicitly ties into the curriculum is your first priority.
### Approaches to Learning (ATL) Integration
ATL skills are the foundational cognitive, research, and self-management skills required of all IB students.
 * **The Skill:** You need to learn how to explicitly map library lessons (like advanced search strings or lateral reading) directly to the **Research** and **Information Literacy** clusters within the ATL framework.
 * **Action:** Don't wait for teachers to come to you. Look at the school's unit plans, see when a research project is coming up, and offer to co-teach the specific ATL skills needed for that task.
### High-Stakes Research Supervision
In an IB secondary school, major independent research projects dictate the upper-grade experience. You will play a crucial role in:
 * **The Extended Essay (EE):** A mandatory 4,000-word independent research paper for DP students. You need to know how to guide students through the early stages of formulating focused research questions and exploring academic databases.
 * **The MYP Personal Project:** A student-driven project in the final year of the MYP. You will need to help students document their research process and track their sources in a process journal.
### Academic Integrity Advocacy
The IB has strict expectations regarding academic honesty.
 * **The Skill:** Moving beyond just acting as the "citation police," you need to teach the ethical use of information. This includes instructing students on how to use AI tools responsibly, understanding intellectual property, and mastering specific citation styles (like MLA or APA) required by your school's DP subjects.
### Mapping to the Learner Profile
A quick and highly visible way to integrate into the school culture is connecting the library's physical space to the **IB Learner Profile** (e.g., *Inquirers, Knowledgeable, Open-Minded*). Curating themed book displays, reading challenges, and bulletin boards around these attributes helps establish the library as the heart of the IB philosophy.
## 2. Essential IB Resources to Master
Navigating the official and unofficial networks of the IB world will save you hours of trial and error.
| Resource Category | Specific Tools & Platforms | What You Use It For |
|---|---|---|
| **Official IB Portals** | **MyIB / Programme Resource Centre (PRC)** | The ultimate destination. This is where you access official subject guides, the *Ideal Libraries* guide, academic integrity policies, and EE coordinator reports. Ask your IB Coordinator for login access on day one. |
| **Cataloging & Collection** | **Follett Destiny & Titlewave** | The industry standard for international school cataloging. Titlewave offers curated **"IB Starter Packs"** and curated curriculum lists specifically mapped to MYP/DP subjects. |
| **Academic Databases** | **JSTOR, EBSCO, Questia, or PressReader** | Critical for DP Internal Assessments (IAs) and the Extended Essay. You need to master these back-end search structures to teach students how to filter peer-reviewed journals vs. monographs. |
| **Professional Networks** | **The Liferarian Association & Follett Webinars** | Global communities of international school library professionals. These networks offer free webinars, shared lesson plans, and collaborative advice specifically tailored to IB library contexts. |
## 3. Immediate Next Steps for Your First Term
If you are looking at your first few weeks on the job, prioritize these operational steps to build momentum smoothly:
 1. Audit the current collection
   Weeks 1-3
   Examine your physical and digital resources. Check the balance between fiction, non-fiction, and digital databases to ensure they support both global perspectives and the specific DP subjects offered at your school.
 2. Build relationships with coordinators
   Weeks 2-4
   Schedule a brief coffee or meeting with your MYP and DP Coordinators. Ask them to share the current year's assessment timelines, unit plans, and areas where they feel students struggle most with research.
 3. Learn the internal assessment (IA) requirements
   Month 2
   Review the guidebooks for heavy research subjects (like History, Global Politics, or Sciences). Understanding the specific source requirements for these internal assessments gives you concrete leverage when approaching teachers to co-teach.
 4. Curate open educational resources (OER)
   Ongoing
   If database budgets are tight, begin building a centralized, easily accessible digital launchpad of credible, free open-access academic repositories, video lectures, and digital archives for student use.
> **A Helpful Mindset Shift:** In an IB school, the library is not just a room full of books; it is an interdependent system of *people, places, collections, and services*. Your most valuable asset isn't the size of your collection, but your willingness to proactively step out from behind the circulation desk and integrate into classroom learning.
