# IB Librarian Professional Development Plan
### Academic Year 2026–2027

---

## How to use this plan
Organized by term so you can pace it alongside the school year rather than trying to do everything at once. Each numbered skill lists **what to build**, the reasoning behind it with a marker like [1] pointing to the source, and a clean **Links** list right underneath.

---

## Pre-Term: Last Week of July (Head Start)

A light orientation week before Term 1 begins — just enough to walk into August with a plan, not a full study load.

- **Day 1–2:** Read the IB "Ideal Libraries" document [1] and the IB Standards & Practices note on the librarian's role [2]. Skim only — you're building a mental map of which practices name your role, not memorizing them yet.
- **Day 3:** Pull your current Destiny collection reports (age, circulation, subject balance) via the Follett Destiny help center if you need a refresher on report tools [3], so you have a baseline before the Term 1 weeding cycle starts.
- **Day 4:** Check the CASIE [4] and IB PD workshop calendars [5] for anything librarian-designated in the Aug–Nov window and get registration on your calendar now, since seats and virtual slots fill up.
- **Day 5:** Skim the CREW manual's introduction and weeding decision matrix [6] so Term 1's weeding work starts from a documented framework rather than ad hoc judgment.

## Term 1 (Aug–Nov): Foundations & Collection Health

### 1. CREW/MUSTIE Weeding & Collection Development
**Build:** A documented weeding cycle tied to your accreditation calendar; a diversity/currency audit of the collection.

*CREW: A Weeding Manual* [1] is the standard reference for a documented weeding decision framework. Follett Destiny's Collection Analysis / Titlewave reports [2] supply the actual age/circulation data to apply it to. IASL [3] publishes ongoing research on collection practice if you want to go deeper than the manual.

[3] [View source ↗](https://iasl-online.org/)

### 2. DDC Cataloging & Follett Destiny Administration
**Build:** Faster, more consistent original cataloging; better use of Destiny's admin/reporting tools.

OCLC's Dewey Services documentation [1] is the reference for cataloging fluency. Follett's own help center and webinar library [2] covers Destiny's admin tools directly. The Follett Community forums [3] are useful for admin-level troubleshooting that the official docs don't cover.

**Links:**

**Build:** A repeatable pre-acquisition screening checklist.

Qatar's Ministry of Education and Higher Education [1] is the primary source, though there isn't one stable page for circulars — check it each term. GCC-region librarian peer networks (often WhatsApp/Facebook groups tied to ISLN Gulf chapters) are useful for cross-checking flagged titles in practice, though these don't have a single public link.

## Term 2 (Nov–Feb): IB-Specific Pedagogy

### 4. Extended Essay & TOK Support
**Build:** A structured EE research-supervision workflow; a TOK resource-curation system tied to knowledge questions.

IB Standards and Practices note that librarians should participate in IB workshops and receive ongoing training on Extended Essay (EE), Approaches to Learning (ATL), and academic integrity [1]. The IB's own PD workshop catalogue [2] includes librarian-specific strands, and CASIE [3], an authorized IB workshop provider, runs recurring virtual/in-person sessions across the year, some explicitly recommended for librarians. Your EE Guide and Coordinator Handbook remain your primary reference and are accessed directly via your school's IBIS/programme resource centre login rather than a public link.

**Links:**
[1] [View source ↗](https://heerubhojwani.com/ib-standards-and-practices-for-librarians-in-myp-and-dp/)
[2] [View source ↗](https://ibo.org/professional-development/)
[3] [View source ↗](https://www.casieonline.org/ib-dp/)

### 5. ATL Skills Instruction & Information Literacy
**Build:** A scope-and-sequence of embedded ATL lessons co-taught with subject teachers across MYP/DP.

IB's "Ideal Libraries" framework positions librarians as providing direct instruction across assessment criteria including goal formation and research skills [1]. The same framework frames librarians as curators of information, catalysts, and connectors [2] — useful language when you're positioning your role with leadership. The JCS Online Resources blog [3] offers practical, IBDP-specific writing on this, worth a periodic read.

**Links:**
[1] [View source ↗](https://ibo.org/ib-world-archive/may-2011-issue-62/between-the-pages/)
[2] [View source ↗](https://jcsonlineresources.org/blog/the-role-of-the-librarian-in-the-ib-dp/)
[3] [View source ↗](https://jcsonlineresources.org/blog/the-role-of-the-librarian-in-the-ib-dp/)

### 6. IB Standards & Practices Literacy
**Build:** Working fluency in the specific practices that name the librarian's role, so you can point to them in evaluations/accreditation prep.

Practice 1.6 covers maintaining a functioning, active library; Practice 2.1 covers compliance with IB-mandated PD requirements; Practice 6.7 covers librarian participation in IB workshops on EE, ATL, and academic integrity [1]. Heeru Bhojwani's writing also maps Standard 2.2 on resource access, 2.3 on inclusion/diversity/equity, and 5.1–5.2 on collaborative planning and resource sharing directly to the librarian's role [2].

**Links:**

[2] [View source ↗](https://heerubhojwani.com/how-to-implement-a-new-ib-school-library-recommendations-for-pyp/)

## Term 3 (Feb–May): Data, Systems & Advocacy

### 7. Data/BI Skills for Reporting
**Build:** Circulation dashboards and collection-diversity reports usable as accreditation evidence.

Note: you've flagged Power BI/PL-900 as a future goal, not started yet — worth scheduling as a discrete study block this term rather than folding it into general PD, since it's a genuinely separate skill track from librarianship. Google's own Apps Script documentation [1] is the reference for scripting Destiny-adjacent or Google Workspace-based automations. Airtable's template library [2] gives you working starting points rather than building from scratch.

## Gap-Fill Addendum (Added July 2026): Closing the Skill & Resource Gaps

A cross-check against the fuller "IB Librarian Essential Skills" competency framework surfaced seven gaps in the terms above. These are additive — slot them into whichever term makes sense for your school year, but Track 10 (AI) and Track 14 (EE 2027 changes) are time-sensitive and worth front-loading.

### 10. Generative AI Literacy & Academic Integrity
**Build:** A school-wide AI literacy workshop; a template for student "AI Statements of Use"; fluency in the IB's three-principle AI framework (Authenticity, Transparency, Integrity).

The IB's guidance treats AI as a potential brainstorming and outlining partner but draws a hard line at AI writing full sentences or fabricating sources [1]. Practical classroom implementation — including a ready-made LibGuide model used in actual EE workshops — is documented by a Florida high school librarian's approach [2]. A CUNY library's AI workshop guide [3] is useful for adapting a student-facing session, and a CSU Los Angeles library workshop series [4] models an ongoing Zoom format you could replicate termly.

### 11. Student Life / Pastoral Care Librarianship
**Build:** A wellbeing-oriented reading collection; a low-sensory reading nook; one inclusive student club tied to the library.

The "Student Life Librarian" archetype treats the library as a third space for pastoral care, not just study — bibliotherapy, safe physical/psychological spaces, and student-led clubs are the core tools [1]. A KQED writeup profiles a working bibliotherapy program with concrete implementation tips (material displays, SEL collaboration with counseling) [2]. Academic literature on bibliotherapy's evidence base and limits is summarized in a Cambridge/ResearchGate review if you want the research grounding before pitching this to admin [3].

**Links:**

### 12. PYP Early Literacy & the Science of Reading
**Build:** Collection-development criteria that account for decodable texts alongside trade books; familiarity with phonological awareness and the Reading Rope model, even if you sit at MYP/DP.

The Science of Reading synthesizes decades of research on how children learn to decode and comprehend text, and directly informs which early-reader titles a library should stock [1]. A school-librarian-specific primer reassures that you don't need a reading-specialist degree to apply the core principles to storytime and collection choices [2].

### 13. Multilingualism & SSST Program Coordination
**Build:** Working knowledge of the School-Supported Self-Taught (SSST) language program structure, so you can support a school's Language Policy and mother-tongue DP students even if you don't coordinate SSST directly.
**Reasoning:** SSST lets DP students study Language A in their mother tongue when the school doesn't offer it as a taught subject, requiring 100–150 hours of self-directed study with an in-school supervisor and an external tutor [1]. The IB's own subject guide sets out supervisor responsibilities in detail [2]. A working LibGuide from an international school shows what student-facing SSST support materials look like in practice [3].

### 14. May 2027 Extended Essay Structural Changes
**Build:** Fluency in the new Dual-Pathway Model (Subject-Focused vs. Interdisciplinary) and the single 500-word Reflective Statement, ahead of supervising your first cohort under the new criteria.

The restructured EE drops from 34 to 30 marks and replaces three separate reflections with one 500-word Reflective Statement emphasizing growth and adaptation [1]. Subject-specific guidance also consolidates from ~30 individual course guides down to 8 unified Subject Group Guidances [2]. A five-part practitioner blog series walks through what's different and what's stayed the same, written specifically for EE coordinators and supervisors [3].

### 15. STEM, Makerspace & Advanced Research Tools
**Build:** Working fluency in a reference manager and at least one AI research assistant; a basic sense of what a school makerspace needs before you pitch or inherit one.

Reference managers like Zotero remove the manual citation-formatting burden and are worth learning even if you only teach them to DP students [1]. AI research assistants (Elicit, scite.ai) help students map scholarly consensus but require the same source-verification instincts as any AI tool [2]. On the physical side, a makerspace needs ventilation, storage, and a clear teaching goal before equipment purchases — a common failure mode is buying a 3D printer without the training and space plan to use it [3].

### 16. External Digital Literacy Frameworks (ISTE & AASL)
**Build:** Familiarity with AASL's Think/Create/Share/Grow framework as a complement to IB's ATL skills, useful both for instructional design and as an advocacy tool with non-IB-trained stakeholders.

AASL's National School Library Standards organize learner competencies into four domains — Think, Create, Share, Grow — that map cleanly onto IB's own inquiry cycle [1]. AASL maintains a growing video library designed explicitly as an introduction-and-advocacy tool for exactly this kind of cross-framework pitch [2].

## Ongoing / Anytime

### Professional Communities Worth Joining
- **IASL** — international, research-oriented, annual conference, small grants for research projects
- **CASIE** — authorized IB PD provider; check their calendar each term for librarian-designated workshops
- **AISL** — more US-independent-school-flavored but strong conference content
- **GCC/Gulf regional librarian networks** — informal but often the fastest source for MoEHE-specific guidance (no stable public link)

**Links:**
[1] IASL — [View source ↗](https://iasl-online.org/)
[2] CASIE — [View source ↗](https://www.casieonline.org/ib-dp/)
[3] AISL — [View source ↗](https://aisl.wildapricot.org/conference)

### A Note on Sequencing
Given your job search is running in parallel, Terms 1–2 content (EE/TOK/ATL fluency, IB standards literacy) is the highest-leverage material to have ready for interviews — it's what differentiates an "IB library specialist" from a generalist. The data/BI track in Term 3 supports your Data Systems Coordinator positioning specifically, so it's worth timing that study block around when you expect interviews for that track to pick up.
