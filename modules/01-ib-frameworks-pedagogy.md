# Integrating Library Skills into the IB Curriculum

To support students and teachers effectively, the librarian has to speak the language of the IB. Understanding how the library explicitly ties into the curriculum is the first priority — and having a second, non-IB vocabulary ready for stakeholders who don't speak that language yet.

## Approaches to Learning (ATL) Integration

ATL skills are the foundational cognitive, research, and self-management skills required of all IB students. The librarian's job is to learn how to explicitly map library lessons — advanced search strings, lateral reading, source evaluation — directly to the **Research** and **Information Literacy** clusters within the ATL framework. The highest-leverage move is proactive rather than reactive: reviewing a school's unit plans, spotting when a research project is coming up, and offering to co-teach the specific ATL skills that task will need, rather than waiting to be invited in.

## IB Standards & Practices Literacy

Working fluency in the specific practices that name the librarian's role gives concrete language to point to in evaluations and accreditation prep. Practice 1.6 covers maintaining a functioning, active library; Practice 2.1 covers compliance with IB-mandated PD requirements; Practice 6.7 covers librarian participation in IB workshops on Extended Essay, ATL, and academic integrity [1]. Complementary practitioner writing maps Standard 2.2 (resource access), 2.3 (inclusion, diversity, and equity), and 5.1–5.2 (collaborative planning and resource sharing) directly onto the librarian's role as well [2].

## External Digital Literacy Frameworks — ISTE & AASL

ATL gives IB-native language for staff conversations, but ISTE and AASL bring something ATL doesn't: pre-built assessment rubrics, tech-integration resources, and — for AASL specifically — advocacy materials aimed at non-education stakeholders. That combination matters when talking to parents, school boards, or accreditation visitors who don't know IB terminology but do recognize "digital citizenship" or "information literacy" as concepts.

The good news is that this crosswalk already exists rather than needing to be built from scratch. AASL has produced a formal **National School Library Standards Crosswalk with ISTE Standards**, mapping ISTE's Standards for Students (2016) to the AASL Standards Frameworks' Learner section, and ISTE's Standards for Educators (2017) to the AASL Standards for School Librarians and School Libraries sections [3]. There's also a librarian-specific **ISTE Standards for Educators / Future Ready Librarians crosswalk**, funded by the Follett Foundation, explicitly framed as a tool for librarians to rethink or grow their role, and for administrators to understand how to support that role [5].

A real three-programme IB school (PYP-MYP-DP) has already done this integration work directly: building its own **Instructional Technology & Information Literacy (ITIL) Standards** by cross-walking IB ATL skills with ISTE Standards for Students and AASL Standards — letting staff use ATL language first in planning conversations with teachers, while still drawing on ISTE's tech-integration resources underneath. The working group that built it included tech integrators, librarians, and the Associate Secondary Principal (who was also Ed Tech Director) — a useful model for who to loop in if attempting something similar [6].

## Video: AASL Standards Series

AASL's video library is built explicitly as an introduction-and-advocacy tool, organized around its **Think, Create, Share, Grow** framework — the same four domains that map cleanly onto IB's own inquiry cycle [4]: [Watch video ↗](https://standards.aasl.org/videos/)

## Bottom line

Rather than adopting ISTE/AASL as a parallel framework to ATL, the highest-leverage move is using the existing AASL-ISTE crosswalk as a reference [3], and considering whether a lightweight version of the IB school's ATL/ISTE/AASL integration exercise [6] would strengthen instructional planning documents ahead of the next accreditation cycle — a natural bridge into Module 7's Accreditation Frameworks page.
