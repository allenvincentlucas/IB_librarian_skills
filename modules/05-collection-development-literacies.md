# Curating an Inclusive and Effective Collection

## Early literacy & PYP: the Science of Reading

Scarborough's Reading Rope, developed by Dr. Hollis Scarborough in 2001, is the dominant visual model in the Science of Reading — it shows skilled reading as the product of two braided strands: **word recognition** (phonological awareness, decoding, sight recognition) and **language comprehension** (background knowledge, vocabulary, language structure, verbal reasoning, literacy knowledge). If even one strand frays, the whole rope weakens — a student can't be a skilled reader on decoding alone, or on comprehension alone [10].

This connects to the earlier **Simple View of Reading** equation (Reading Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension) — Scarborough's model breaks that equation into specific, teachable sub-skills, which is what makes it usable for collection-development decisions [8]. The direct implication: decodable texts (books with controlled, phonics-aligned vocabulary) build the lower/word-recognition strand, while trade books and read-alouds build the upper/comprehension strand — a PYP library needs both, not one instead of the other [7].

A primer aimed directly at library staff reassures that a reading-specialist degree isn't required to apply these principles to storytime and collection choices — the core skills show up naturally in well-run read-alouds and rhyme/song activities [1].

**IB/PYP-specific framing:** PYP librarians connect IB learner profile attributes, transdisciplinary skills, and concepts to stories and the Units of Inquiry, working through read-alouds guided by "Reading with a Purpose" and "five keys to storytelling" [3]. A companion resource collects the actual **IB accreditation assessor questions** a PYP librarian may be asked — directly useful for evaluation prep: how the library supports early literacy, integrates visual literacy and storytelling, and provides multilingual resources aligned with the school's language policy [2]. PYP units are organized under six transdisciplinary themes via each school's own Programme of Inquiry (PoI); the library's job is to source read-alouds that reinforce whichever theme is currently in focus rather than running library sessions as a stand-alone subject [6]. A working blog collects specific read-aloud recommendations mapped to PYP units of inquiry, useful as a starting list rather than building one from scratch [9].

**Global/translated literature for international-mindedness:** incorporating global literature — Nathan Kumar Scott's *Mangoes and Bananas*, Mahasweta Devi's *The Why-Why Girl*, Zai Whitaker's *Kali and the Rat Snake* — helps model IB Learner Profile attributes (open-minded, principled, reflective) through story rather than direct instruction.

## Multilingualism & SSST program coordination

The IB's own **SSST student guide** states directly that librarians may help SSST students compile a booklist, carry out research, and discuss literature — this isn't an inferred responsibility, it's written into the primary source document [19]. Under Practice 1.1, the librarian's role is to learn and apply the school's Language Policy — providing multilingual resources, organizing storytelling sessions in multiple languages, and building a collection reflecting the community's cultural backgrounds [12].

An SSST course lets a DP student study Language A: Literature in a language the school doesn't formally teach, with an in-school supervisor (a DP Language A teacher) plus an external tutor for first-language support — the school's job is coordination and academic oversight, not instruction itself [15]. Recent updates reduced the required works from nine to seven over the two years, with at least four originally written in the study language and three in translation, all drawn from the IB's Prescribed Reading List [14] [18].

Schools sometimes outsource the whole coordination burden: **Pamoja Education** offers a fully-managed SSST Language A: Literature pathway [16], and a boutique **SSST Coordinator** service runs the logistics for schools that don't want to build this in-house [17].

**Video:** Introductory video about SSST from the IBO itself — overview from a teacher's perspective and student expectations [13]: [Watch video ↗](https://player.vimeo.com/video/309488012)

Given the librarian is not typically the SSST supervisor, the highest-leverage move isn't becoming a language tutor — it's positioning the library as the **resource-and-booklist support role** the IB guide already names [19], and making sure Language Policy documentation reflects multilingual storytelling and collection diversity per Practice 1.1 [12].

## Collection health: weeding, cataloging, and compliance

A documented weeding cycle tied to the accreditation calendar, plus a diversity/currency audit, starts with **CREW: A Weeding Manual** — the standard reference for a documented decision framework [20]. Follett Destiny's Collection Analysis and Titlewave reports supply the actual age/circulation data to apply it to [21], and IASL publishes ongoing research on collection practice for anyone wanting to go deeper than the manual [22].

For cataloging fluency, OCLC's Dewey Services documentation is the reference point [23]; Follett's own help center and webinar library covers Destiny's admin tools directly [21], and the Follett Community forums are useful for admin-level troubleshooting the official docs don't cover [24]. For pre-acquisition screening in a Gulf/GCC context specifically, the relevant Ministry of Education and Higher Education is the primary source, though circulars don't sit on one stable page — worth checking each term [25]; regional librarian peer networks (often informal WhatsApp/Facebook groups) are useful for cross-checking flagged titles in practice.
