# The Library as a "Third Place"

## The theoretical grounding

The framing that keeps showing up in the literature is Oldenburg's **Third Place** concept, applied to school libraries — openly accessible, free, welcoming without prejudice, low-stress, loosely structured, interactive, and collaborative. Librarians need to actively adapt their role and space design to cultivate those qualities on purpose [8].

National reading-and-wellbeing survey data found that students who don't use the school library are nearly twice as likely to report low mental wellbeing as high wellbeing — correlational, not causal. Separately, design-focused research found students specifically valued interactive, learner-centered, inclusive, and flexible library spaces as contributing directly to their sense of wellbeing, not just their study outcomes [3]. A first-person account worth keeping for staff conversations: a former student describes how a librarian who remembered her name after one visit became, over time, "the adult I trusted most in my school building" [13].

## The librarian–counselor partnership model

The most directly useful find is a **Berlin Brandenburg International School** case study — an actual IB school running a monthly primary-library session co-led by the librarian and school counselor, built around wellbeing themes (feelings, relationships, IB Learner Profile attributes), which they call their own form of bibliotherapy. Especially well received by newcomers and students with learning difficulties [10].

A smaller-scale U.S. elementary example: a "Counselor's Corner" shelf in the library, stocked by the counselor with SEL-themed titles, requiring almost no extra staff time [11]. A webinar specifically on librarian–counselor collaboration around bibliotherapy features school librarian Ida Mae Craddock and school counselor Ouida Powe [9].

## Physical space: low-sensory rooms

Growing practice in academic/public libraries, less common yet in K-12 but well-documented enough to borrow from:

- **NYU Libraries** — sensory pod, high-backed chairs, bean bags, adjustable lighting, noise-canceling wall paneling, sized for 12–14 students [5].
- **Penn State's Vairo Library** — lower-budget version: dimmable forest-themed lighting, weighted lap blankets, fidget toys, noise-canceling headphones, tabletop zen garden [7].
- **VCU Libraries** sensory room case study [12].
- **Georgia State University** sensory room opening [2].
- A practical guide distinguishing **sensory-seeking** (colorful, fidget toys, floor seating) vs. **sensory-avoidant** (quiet, dim, no strong smells) space design, with a real budget-tiered case study [1].

## Inclusive clubs — "Blokes with Books"

Confirmed as a real, currently-running club: an international-school teacher-librarian runs an ongoing "BWB — Blokes with Books" club for reluctant boy readers, using tools like a genre-personality quiz, with reported spontaneous wellbeing outcomes — boys describing reading as calming when angry or upset — plus a knock-on effect of drawing faculty into YA reading [4].

## Bottom line

Three deliverables close this gap: (1) a pitch to the school counselor for a monthly bibliotherapy session modeled on the Berlin Brandenburg example [10], (2) a modest low-sensory corner using the Penn State-style budget list rather than NYU's [7], and (3) reviving or adapting a "Blokes with Books"-style reluctant-reader club if the school doesn't already have one [4].
