# Supervising the Research Process (MYP & DP)

Major independent research projects dictate the upper-grade experience in an IB school. This module covers the two capstone projects the librarian supports most directly — the MYP Personal Project and the DP Extended Essay — the tools that support them, and a developmental map for building research skills toward both.

## The MYP Personal Project

The Personal Project is a student-driven project in the final year of the MYP, and the librarian's role centers on documentation and source discipline rather than instruction: helping students track their research process and evaluate sources in a process journal. The IB's own guidance frames the supervisor's job as monitoring progress through conversation, written interactions, and process-journal review, while confirming the authenticity of the final work [9]. A working librarian-facing LibGuide from an international school lays out the supervisor's specific responsibilities in practice: confirming the topic satisfies ethical and safety standards, guiding students through the process, and participating in the school's assessment standardization [10]. The same "culminating project" framing the IB itself uses for Personal Project, PYP Exhibition, and Extended Essay makes clear the librarian's role scales up across all three — research mentor during PYP Exhibition, documentation coach during Personal Project, source-evaluation specialist by the Extended Essay [11].

## May 2027 Extended Essay Structural Changes

Confirmed directly from the IB's own guide: interdisciplinary Extended Essays require students to integrate the knowledge, concepts, theories, perspectives, or methods of two DP subjects, assessed using the Subject-Specific Guidance for whichever two subject areas the student chose [2]. Students choosing the interdisciplinary pathway must register their essay under exactly one of five frameworks:

1. **Culture, identity, expression**
2. **Digital society and design technology**
3. **Power, equality, justice**
4. **Sustainability, development, change**
5. **Economics and global politics**

A topic doesn't need to touch every word in its chosen framework — for "culture, identity, expression," a student could focus purely on identity. The framework choice isn't directly assessed; it exists mainly to guide the student's inquiry and help the IB route the essay to appropriate examiners [7].

Beyond the 34→30 mark reduction, the previous standalone Criterion D (Presentation) has been phased out entirely, with its elements folded into the new Criterion A (Framework for the essay) — so presentation is no longer a separate judgment call, it's baked into how well the essay is framed. Subject-specific guidance also consolidates from roughly 30 individual course guides down to 8 unified Subject Group Guidances [1]. The same five criteria (A–E) now assess both pathways equally, with pathway-specific requirements built into how Criteria A and B are applied rather than having separate criteria per pathway [4].

A useful pre-supervision move given the frameworks system: have students "take their idea for a walk," testing whether their topic could sit in more than one framework depending on which angle of the research question they emphasize — the same topic (e.g., gender) could land under "power, equality, justice" or "culture, identity, expression" depending on emphasis. That ambiguity is by design, not something to resolve too early [3].

## Research Skills Scaffolding Matrix

Scaffolding research skills across a seven-year secondary continuum means moving students systematically from **guided consumption** to **independent, ethical production**. This map anchors directly to the **Research** category of ATL, broken into *Information Literacy* and *Media Literacy*.

| Programme Stage | Cognitive Focus | Library Intervention Target | Key Mastery Indicators |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** *(MYP 1–2)* | Gathering & Verifying | Keyword formulation, catalog searching, basic bias identification, intro to a citation tool | Separates keywords from natural language; distinguishes a blog from an encyclopedia; builds a basic bibliography entry |
| **Intermediate** *(MYP 3–4)* | Evaluating & Organizing | Advanced search strings, basic school databases, CRAAP evaluation, unintentional plagiarism | Uses boolean operators; filters by domain/date; incorporates simple in-text citations and paraphrasing |
| **Capstone Transition** *(MYP 5)* | Formulating & Synthesizing | Narrow research questions, initial academic search, mapping data to arguments for the Personal Project | Moves from broad topic to focused question; synthesizes 3+ source types into a cohesive bibliography |
| **Advanced Research** *(DP 1)* | Analyzing & Critiquing | Peer-reviewed networks (JSTOR/EBSCO), deep-dive citation formatting, methodological limitations for Internal Assessments | Evaluates author authority; manages a multi-page works cited page; extracts data trends from studies |
| **Independent Mastery** *(DP 2)* | Defending & Constructing | Complex bibliographies, niche source citations, copyright/AI ethics for the Extended Essay | Operates independently in digital archives; self-corrects complex citations; applies integrity principles to final drafts |

**Four implementation strategies:**

1. **Unified citation & note-taking templates.** Standardize a single citation tool (NoodleTools, MyBib, Zotero) and note-taking method (Cornell System, digital matrices) school-wide, from MYP 1 through DP 2, to cut cognitive load from shifting formatting expectations.
2. **The "Research Question" diagnostic (MYP 5 → DP 1).** Run a mandatory workshop at the start of the Personal Project and Extended Essay cycles using a funneling template: Interest → Topic → Focused Question → Working Argument.
3. **Database scaffolding.** MYP 1–3 gets curated platforms (Britannica School, World Book); MYP 4–5 gets general academic search (Google Scholar, Gale); DP 1–2 transitions fully to JSTOR/EBSCO, with explicit instruction on reading an abstract before committing to a 30-page paper.
4. **Co-planned, just-in-time interventions.** Skip stand-alone orientation lessons in September — they're forgotten by November. Check the assessment calendar and schedule library workshops the week a specific assessment demands them.

By DP 2, the librarian's role should have shifted from teaching basic mechanics to acting as a scholarly consultant helping students refine arguments, locate niche materials, and navigate intellectual property questions.

## Advanced Research Tools: Elicit, Scite, and Zotero

The distinction worth teaching students explicitly: Elicit searches a database of roughly 138 million real academic papers and links every answer back to its source paper with supporting quotes, whereas general-purpose AI chat tools generate from training data and can produce false citations — Elicit is built specifically for research [13]. Its core features are worth knowing before teaching it: Paper Researcher (natural-language search), Data Extractor (pulls data points into tables), Notebook (chat with saved papers), and Research Reports (auto-drafts literature reviews) — though Elicit can miss up to 15% of relevant papers, so it should pair with a traditional database search rather than replace one [13].

**Scite** does something different and complementary: rather than finding papers, it shows *how* a paper has been cited elsewhere — supporting, contrasting, or merely mentioning — which teaches students to check whether a source is well-regarded in its field rather than just citation-count-popular [14]. Reference managers like **Zotero** remove the manual citation-formatting burden entirely and are worth learning even if only teaching them to DP students [15].

## Bottom line

Given no makerspace or research-tools programme exists yet in most schools starting from scratch, teaching Elicit and Scite together (one finds papers, one checks their credibility) is a stronger pairing than either alone [13] [14]. The frameworks list for the 2027 Extended Essay [7] is worth turning into a quick-reference handout for the first EE info session — students need to pick one before registering.
