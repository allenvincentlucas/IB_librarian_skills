# Navigating AI and Ethics in the IB

## What the IB actually requires

The core document is the IB's **Academic Integrity Policy**, with a dedicated appendix titled *Guidance on the Use of Artificial Intelligence Tools*. Any AI-generated content included in assessed work must be credited in the body of the text and referenced in the bibliography, and students need to be taught to recognize the bias baked into AI output rather than accept it uncritically [5].

The IB has been explicit for years that it will **not ban** AI tools — it treats AI as inevitable infrastructure, comparing it to spell checkers, translation software, and calculators, and prefers to teach ethical use over prohibition [6]. On citation mechanics, the IB's own FAQ is direct: it isn't claiming AI is a source in the traditional sense, but is seeking transparency [6]. Practically, that means the citation should include the tool used, the prompt given to it, and the date it generated the response — functionally the same shape as MLA's own generative-AI citation guidance [10].

## Where the librarian specifically fits in

A 2025 qualitative study of international-school librarians found that despite drawbacks, students already treat generative AI as a primary information-seeking source, and librarians consistently identify fact-checking as the essential skill to teach around it [15]. Zakir Hossain, IASL Vice President, developed an **AI Citizenship Framework** specifically built for teacher-librarians, with ready-to-use curriculum plans integrating AI literacy from an ethical, critical, and societal perspective [4]. A follow-up international survey of school librarians found the real bottleneck isn't willingness — it's capacity: limited time, lack of instructional materials, and minimal training are the critical barriers [13].

## What "good" looks like in practice

At **Suncoast Community High School** in Florida, a librarian became the AI-literacy lead for Extended Essay supervision specifically, building a public LibGuide covering brainstorming, research, writing, and citation with AI, used across a two-day EE workshop with juniors [12]. At **John Street School**, librarian Paige Chambers uses AI tools herself — lesson planning, summarizing video content — before teaching them to students, a "model it yourself first" approach [14].

## Citation mechanics reference

International School of Kenya uses a quotation-mark plus prompt/date citation format, paired with a short academic honesty video [9]. International School of Beijing documents the EE authentication process alongside AI use [8]. Canadian International School Singapore offers a practical Dos/Don'ts list for EE and AI use [1]. All three mirror the underlying format set out in MLA's own generative-AI citation guidance [10].

## Actionable implementation

Three concrete deliverables close this gap: (1) a one-page AI Statement of Use template for students, built off the IB's own citation guidance [6], (2) a staff PD session built around the Glanville clips and FAQ [3], and (3) adapting Hossain's AI Citizenship Framework rather than building instruction from scratch [4] — all organized around the IB's core principles of **Authenticity, Transparency, and Integrity**.
