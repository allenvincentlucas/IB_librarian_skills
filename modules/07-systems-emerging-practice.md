# Systems, Compliance, and Emerging Practice

Four areas that sit outside day-to-day pedagogy but shape how the library actually runs: the system you manage the collection through, the legal boundaries around what you can reuse, the accreditation frameworks alongside IB authorization, and how AI itself fits into the librarian's own workflow rather than just the classroom.

## Follett Destiny Administration

Destiny Library Manager now sits inside a broader Library Suite alongside Destiny District Classroom Library Manager, which extends resource tracking from the central library into individual classroom collections, and Destiny Engage, which layers on personalized reading recommendations and challenges [1]. For an IB librarian juggling a central secondary collection plus subject-department classroom sets, the Classroom Library Manager piece is worth learning even if it wasn't part of the original rollout.

An AI capability now built directly into the platform can automate parts of reporting and surface collection insights automatically — worth testing against an existing Power BI dashboard rather than treating the two as competing tools [2]. District-level dashboards give both librarians and administrators shared visibility into usage metrics, useful language for Learning Resources Committee reporting [3].

**Titlewave and TitleWise** form the collection-analysis engine: TitleWise is Follett's free collection-analysis tool, reachable directly from Destiny's Back Office, producing custom reports on collection age, subject balance, and circulation health rather than requiring a separate weeding audit built from scratch [4]. The workflow: submit MARC records to Titlewave from Destiny's Catalog menu, then run the TitleWise Analysis from the Titlewave account [5]. Once uploaded, Titlewave's List tool supports sharing curated lists with colleagues, tagging "Do Not Exceed" budget notes directly on a list, and comparing a proposed list against actual student population data before ordering [6].

A short Follett-produced walkthrough shows the one-click path from Destiny into Titlewave [7], and a working district library guide recommends running the TitleWise analysis to target weeding priorities throughout the year rather than saving it for a single annual audit [8].

**Bottom line:** a scheduled quarterly TitleWise re-analysis (rather than once a year, timed to the Term 1 weeding cycle in [Module 6]({{ '/modules/06-systems-makerspaces-pd-timeline/' | relative_url }})), a shared Titlewave list workflow for subject-department requests, and a side-by-side test of Destiny's built-in AI reporting against any existing BI dashboard to see whether one can be retired.

## Copyright & Fair Use for International School Libraries

U.S. copyright law's Section 107 sets out four factors for judging fair use: purpose and character of the use, the nature of the copyrighted work, how much is used relative to the whole, and the effect on the work's potential market [9]. No fixed word count or percentage makes a use automatically safe — every factor has to be weighed together against the specific situation [17].

For international schools specifically, this framework travels as informal best practice even outside the U.S.: one Gulf-region university library guide explicitly applies U.S. fair use doctrine "within the framework of international copyright conventions" while checking it against local statutes — a workable model for a GCC-based IB school without its own codified copyright policy [12].

The **TEACH Act** specifically governs digital transmission and display of copyrighted works in distance or online-enhanced teaching, but it does not cover posting text materials like PDFs of full articles or chapters — that still falls under the general fair use test or a separate classroom-use exception limited to face-to-face instruction [10]. Practically: a teacher can display a poem or short film clip live in a lesson under TEACH Act protections, but uploading the same PDF to a shared drive for asynchronous access needs its own fair use analysis [11].

**Creative Commons** licensing gives a standardized way for creators to grant reuse permissions while keeping their copyright — the most teachable middle ground between "public domain" and "fully restricted" for MYP and DP students building multimedia projects [16]. A ready-made classroom activity has students search for and correctly attribute a Creative Commons image, extending the same citation instinct already used for text sources to images, audio, and video [14]. Age-appropriate, pre-filtered CC image sources exist specifically for K-12 use [15], and a comprehensive reference guide works through license anatomy, open pedagogy, and open educational resources chapter by chapter for a deeper staff PD session [13].

**Bottom line:** a one-page, GCC-appropriate fair use and Creative Commons quick-reference for staff; a clear internal rule distinguishing "shown live in class" from "posted online" for any copyrighted text or media; and a standing list of pre-vetted CC/public-domain sources for MYP/DP student multimedia projects.

## Accreditation Frameworks (CIS, NEASC, WASC)

The IB authorization process is functionally equivalent to accreditation for PYP, MYP, DP, and CP specifically, but it doesn't evaluate the whole school the way a general accreditor does — that's the gap CIS, NEASC, and WASC fill [21]. Many international schools hold both: IB authorization for programme delivery, plus whole-school accreditation from one or more regional bodies [28].

**Council of International Schools (CIS)** partners directly with the U.S. regional accreditors — NEASC, WASC, and MSA — using a jointly developed protocol so a school can pursue CIS and regional U.S. accreditation in a single combined visit [18]. **NEASC**, through its Commission on International Education, accredits schools worldwide from early childhood through secondary, and is especially common among American-curriculum international schools [22]. **WASC** has a strong international portfolio concentrated in Asia and the Middle East, including many well-known IB schools in the Gulf [21].

Worth knowing before assuming accreditation documentation will do the work for you: a review of multiple accreditors' published standards found library-specific language is often thin and generic — phrasing like "library resources and services are adequate" rather than concrete expectations [19]. In practice, the librarian usually has to build the evidence case for their own role rather than pointing to a pre-written standard, which is exactly why the *Ideal Libraries* document (referenced in [Module 6]({{ '/modules/06-systems-makerspaces-pd-timeline/' | relative_url }})) functions as an advocacy tool rather than a compliance checklist.

NEASC's Foundation Standards are the baseline, compliance-oriented requirements every accredited school must evidence from day one, reviewed in depth roughly every four to five years alongside required Annual Updates in between [23] [24]. A school moving between accreditors goes through a two-day Foundation Standards Visit, and NEASC will generally honor the school's existing accreditation cycle from another body rather than starting the clock over [20].

**Bottom line:** confirm which accreditor(s) the school currently holds and where it sits in that four-to-five-year visit cycle, since library evidence-gathering should ramp up roughly a year ahead of a full Visit; build a library-specific evidence file now rather than waiting for a standard to name it explicitly [19]; keep the *Ideal Libraries* advocacy framing on hand specifically for accreditation self-study writing.

## Using Claude / AI as a Librarian Workflow Tool

A useful reframe for staff who are skeptical of AI tools: helping people evaluate provenance, judge source authority, and decide what to trust are questions librarians have answered for over a century, just applied to a new kind of output [27]. That reframe matters when introducing Claude to colleagues who see AI adoption as a separate "tech skill" rather than an extension of the professional judgment they already have.

A practitioner walkthrough of AI tool rollout in libraries identifies staff resistance and workflow disruption with existing systems as the two most common failure points, and stresses that a tool needs a clear, narrow operational brief rather than being deployed as an open-ended assistant [26]. In practice this means writing a specific, reusable prompt for a specific task — a book-display blurb, a Titlewave list description, a parent-newsletter summary — rather than treating every use as a one-off conversation. Concrete staff-level applications worth piloting first: drafting first-pass marketing copy for events and reading challenges, experimenting with AI-assisted subject-heading suggestions for a cataloging backlog (treated as a draft requiring professional verification, not a final answer), and drafting grant proposal language later refined with real institutional context [27].

Reusable prompt libraries beat one-off requests. Anthropic maintains a public library of ready-to-copy prompts, organized around a repeatable pattern: describe the outcome wanted, not the exact steps, and let the tool figure out the path [25]. The same logic applies directly to library work — a PD session for staff is far more useful if it produces a shared bank of tested prompts than if it just demonstrates the tool once and moves on. A specifically librarian-facing example built a themed set of AI prompts and templates for reader-advisory lists, book displays, and event promotion [29], and a broader K-12 prompt library for lesson planning and administrative tasks is a useful comparison point [30].

**Bottom line:** a shared, versioned prompt library specific to library operations rather than leaving each staff member to reinvent prompts individually; an explicit "draft, don't finalize" rule for any AI-assisted cataloging or subject-heading work, matching the verification standard already used for AI-assisted student research; and framing AI-literacy PD for library staff around the reframe above — this is an extension of professional judgment already held, not a separate technical skill to be anxious about.
