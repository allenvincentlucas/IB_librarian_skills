# Gap 7: External Digital Literacy Frameworks — ISTE & AASL

*Research notes for the Gap-Fill Addendum (Track 16) — added to the vault July 2026. In-text citations follow MLA 9th edition, with a bracketed number [n] alongside each for quick lookup against the numbered Works Cited list at the end.*

## 1. The official crosswalk document exists — no need to build one from scratch

AASL has already produced a formal **National School Library Standards Crosswalk with ISTE Standards**, mapping ISTE's Standards for Students (2016) to the AASL Standards Frameworks' Learner section, and ISTE's Standards for Educators (2017) to the AASL Standards for School Librarians and School Libraries sections (American Association of School Librarians, *National School Library Standards Crosswalk* [1]).

There's also a librarian-specific **ISTE Standards for Educators / Future Ready Librarians crosswalk**, funded by the Follett Foundation, explicitly framed as a tool for librarians to rethink or grow their role, and for administrators to understand how to support that role (International Society for Technology in Education [3]).

## 2. A real IB school already did the ATL/ISTE/AASL integration work

This is the strongest find for this specific context: a three-programme IB school (PYP-MYP-DP) built its own **Instructional Technology & Information Literacy (ITIL) Standards** by cross-walking IB ATL skills with ISTE Standards for Students and AASL Standards, explicitly to let staff use ATL language first in planning conversations with teachers while still drawing on ISTE's tech-integration resources underneath. The working group included tech integrators, librarians, and the Associate Secondary Principal, who was also Ed Tech Director — a useful model for who to loop in if attempting something similar (Sjtylr [4]).

## 3. Why bother with ISTE/AASL if ATL already exists

The practical case: ATL gives IB-native language for staff conversations, but ISTE and AASL bring pre-built assessment rubrics, tech-integration resources, and (for AASL specifically) advocacy materials aimed at non-education stakeholders — useful when talking to parents, school boards, or accreditation visitors who don't know IB terminology but do recognize "digital citizenship" or "information literacy" as concepts.

## Video references

- **AASL Standards video series** (Think, Create, Share, Grow — introductory and advocacy use) (American Association of School Librarians, "Videos" [2]): [View source ↗](https://standards.aasl.org/videos/)

*Usage note: no dedicated public video specifically explaining the ISTE/AASL crosswalk was found — the two PDF crosswalk documents (American Association of School Librarians [1]; International Society for Technology in Education [3]) are the primary reference material for this gap rather than video.*

## Bottom line

Rather than adopting ISTE/AASL as a parallel framework, the highest-leverage move is using the existing AASL-ISTE crosswalk PDF as a reference (American Association of School Librarians [1]), and considering whether a lightweight version of the IB school's ATL/ISTE/AASL integration exercise (Sjtylr [4]) would strengthen instructional planning documents ahead of the next accreditation cycle.
