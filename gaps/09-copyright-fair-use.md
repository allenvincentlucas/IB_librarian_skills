# Gap 9: Copyright & Fair Use for International School Libraries

*New research added for the IB Librarian Skills website, July 2026. In-text citations follow MLA 9th edition, with a bracketed number [n] alongside each for quick lookup against the numbered Works Cited list at the end.*

## 1. The four-factor fair use test

U.S. copyright law's Section 107 sets out four factors for judging whether a use of copyrighted material counts as fair use: the purpose and character of the use (commercial versus nonprofit educational), the nature of the copyrighted work itself, how much of the work is used relative to the whole, and the effect of the use on the work's potential market (University of North Georgia Libraries [1]). No fixed word count or percentage makes a use automatically safe — each factor has to be weighed together against the specific situation (Medical University of South Carolina Libraries [via MECC Library Guide, 9]).

For international schools specifically, this framework travels as informal best practice even outside the U.S.: American University in Dubai's library guide explicitly applies U.S. fair use doctrine "within the framework of international copyright conventions" while checking it against local UAE statutes, which is a workable model for a GCC-based IB school without its own codified copyright policy (American University in Dubai [4]).

## 2. The TEACH Act covers online instruction, not print handouts

The TEACH Act specifically governs digital transmission and display of copyrighted works in distance or online-enhanced teaching, but it does not cover posting text materials like PDFs of full articles or chapters — that still falls under the general fair use test or a separate classroom-use exception limited to face-to-face instruction (Chapman University Libraries [2]). Practically: a teacher can display a poem or short film clip live in a lesson under TEACH Act protections, but uploading the same PDF to a shared drive for asynchronous student access needs its own fair use analysis (University of Southern California Libraries [3]).

## 3. Creative Commons as the practical default for student and staff work

Creative Commons licensing gives a standardized way for creators to grant reuse permissions while keeping their copyright, which makes it the most teachable middle ground between "public domain" and "fully restricted" for MYP and DP students building multimedia projects (Ohio State University Teaching and Learning Resource Center [8]). A ready-made classroom activity has students search for and correctly attribute a Creative Commons image, extending the same citation instinct they already use for text sources to images, audio, and video (University of Delaware Teaching and Learning Services [6]).

Age-appropriate, pre-filtered CC image sources exist specifically for K-12 use, removing the guesswork of vetting a general search result for classroom safety (Edutopia [7]). A comprehensive reference guide (Benson, *Creative Commons for Educators and Librarians*) works through license anatomy, open pedagogy, and open educational resources chapter by chapter if a deeper staff PD session is wanted (ALA Store [5]).

## Bottom line

Three deliverables would close this gap: (1) a one-page, GCC-appropriate fair use and Creative Commons quick-reference for staff, modeled on the American University in Dubai guide rather than a straight U.S. academic library template, (2) a clear internal rule distinguishing "shown live in class" from "posted online" for any copyrighted text or media, since only the first is safely covered without a fair use review, and (3) a standing list of pre-vetted CC/public-domain image and media sources for MYP/DP student multimedia projects.
