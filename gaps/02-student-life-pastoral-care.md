# Gap 2: Student Life / Pastoral Care Librarianship

*Research notes for the Gap-Fill Addendum (Track 11) — added to the vault July 2026. In-text citations follow MLA 9th edition, with a bracketed number [n] alongside each for quick lookup against the numbered Works Cited list at the end.*

## 1. The theoretical grounding: the library as "Third Place"

The framing that keeps showing up in the literature is Oldenburg's **Third Place** concept, applied to school libraries — openly accessible, free, welcoming without prejudice, low-stress, loosely structured, interactive, and collaborative. The argument is that librarians need to actively adapt their role and space design to cultivate those qualities on purpose (Raffaele [8]).

National reading-and-wellbeing survey data found that students who don't use the school library are nearly twice as likely to report low mental wellbeing as high wellbeing — correlational, not causal. Separately, design-focused research found students specifically valued interactive, learner-centered, inclusive, and flexible library spaces as contributing directly to their sense of wellbeing, not just their study outcomes ("Media Literacy in the Third Space School Library" [3]).

A first-person account worth keeping for framing conversations with staff: a former student describes how a librarian who remembered her name after one visit became, over time, "the adult I trusted most in my school building" (Youth Today [13]).

## 2. The librarian–counselor partnership model

The most directly useful find is a **Berlin Brandenburg International School** case study — an actual IB school running a monthly primary-library session co-led by the librarian and school counselor, built around wellbeing themes (feelings, relationships, IB Learner Profile attributes), which they call their own form of bibliotherapy. Especially well received by newcomers and students with learning difficulties (The International Educator [10]).

A smaller-scale U.S. elementary example — a "Counselor's Corner" shelf in the library, stocked by the counselor with SEL-themed titles, requiring almost no extra staff time (The Responsive Counselor [11]).

A webinar specifically on librarian–counselor collaboration around bibliotherapy, featuring school librarian Ida Mae Craddock and school counselor Ouida Powe (School Library Connection [9]).

## 3. Physical space: low-sensory rooms

Growing practice in academic/public libraries, less common yet in K-12 but well-documented enough to borrow from:

- **NYU Libraries** — sensory pod, high-backed chairs, bean bags, adjustable lighting, noise-canceling wall paneling, sized for 12–14 students (New York University [5]).
- **Penn State's Vairo Library** — lower-budget version: dimmable forest-themed lighting, weighted lap blankets, fidget toys, noise-canceling headphones, tabletop zen garden (Penn State University [7]).
- **VCU Libraries** sensory room case study (Virginia Commonwealth University [12]).
- **Georgia State University** sensory room opening (Georgia State University [2]).
- Practical guide distinguishing **sensory-seeking** (colorful, fidget toys, floor seating) vs. **sensory-avoidant** (quiet, dim, no strong smells) space design, with a real budget-tiered case study from Montana State University (Frank and Pawlak [1]).

## 4. Inclusive clubs — "Blokes with Books"

Confirmed as a real, currently-running club: an international-school teacher-librarian runs an ongoing "BWB — Blokes with Books" club for reluctant boy readers, using tools like a genre-personality quiz, with reported spontaneous wellbeing outcomes — boys describing reading as calming when angry or upset — plus a knock-on effect of drawing faculty into YA reading (Nadine [4]).

## Video references

- **"Bibliotherapy!"** (Ji Young Park [6]) — short, accessible explainer on how bibliotherapy works as a concept: [Watch video ↗](https://youtu.be/8wTWltJRgUk)

*Usage note: this is informal, personally-posted content — fine to link as an accessible on-ramp, but pair with the Berlin Brandenburg case study (The International Educator [10]) or peer-reviewed bibliotherapy research if you need something with more institutional weight (e.g. for an accreditation document or formal PD proposal).*

No strong standalone video was found for sensory-room tours — coverage is almost entirely written case studies. A direct YouTube search for "library sensory room tour" may surface campus reveal clips that don't show up well in web search.

## Bottom line

Three deliverables would close this gap: (1) a pitch to the school counselor for a monthly bibliotherapy session modeled on the Berlin Brandenburg example (The International Educator [10]), (2) a modest low-sensory corner using the Penn State-style budget list rather than NYU's (Penn State University [7]), and (3) reviving or adapting a "Blokes with Books"-style reluctant-reader club if the school doesn't already have one (Nadine [4]).
