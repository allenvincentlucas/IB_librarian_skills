# Gap 3: PYP Early Literacy & the Science of Reading

*Research notes for the Gap-Fill Addendum (Track 12) — added to the vault July 2026. In-text citations follow MLA 9th edition, with a bracketed number [n] alongside each for quick lookup against the numbered Works Cited list at the end.*

## 1. The core framework: Scarborough's Reading Rope

Scarborough's Reading Rope, developed by Dr. Hollis Scarborough in 2001, is the dominant visual model in the Science of Reading — it shows skilled reading as the product of two braided strands: **word recognition** (phonological awareness, decoding, sight recognition) and **language comprehension** (background knowledge, vocabulary, language structure, verbal reasoning, literacy knowledge). The metaphor's key teaching point: if even one strand frays, the whole rope weakens — a student can't be a skilled reader on decoding alone, or on comprehension alone (Reading.com [10]).

This connects to the earlier **Simple View of Reading** equation (Reading Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension) — Scarborough's model breaks that equation down into specific, teachable sub-skills, which is what makes it usable for collection-development decisions (Landmark Outreach [8]).

**Direct collection-development implication:** decodable texts (books with controlled, phonics-aligned vocabulary) build the lower/word-recognition strand, while trade books and read-alouds build the upper/comprehension strand — a PYP library needs both, not one instead of the other (Just Right Reader [7]).

## 2. The librarian's role specifically (not just a classroom teacher's job)

A primer aimed directly at library staff reassures that you don't need a reading-specialist degree to apply these principles to storytime and collection choices — the core skills (phonological awareness, decoding) show up naturally in well-run read-alouds and rhyme/song activities (Association for Library Service to Children [1]).

## 3. IB/PYP-specific framing

This is the most directly useful find for this context — Heeru Bhojwani's PYP librarian writing gives a concrete picture of the role: PYP librarians connect IB learner profile attributes, transdisciplinary skills, and concepts to stories and the Units of Inquiry, working through read-alouds guided by "Reading with a Purpose" and "five keys to storytelling" (Bhojwani, "PYP Librarians" [3]).

A companion piece collects the actual **IB accreditation assessor questions** a PYP librarian may be asked — directly useful for evaluation prep: "How does the library support early literacy for younger students?", "How do you integrate visual literacy, storytelling, and picture books into learning experiences?", "How does the library provide resources in multiple languages to support the school's language policy?" (Bhojwani, "IB Assessor Questions" [2]).

On the transdisciplinary structure itself: PYP units are organized under six transdisciplinary themes via each school's own Programme of Inquiry (PoI), and the library's job is to source read-alouds and resources that reinforce whichever theme is currently in focus rather than running library sessions as a stand-alone subject (International Baccalaureate Organization [6]).

A working blog collects specific read-aloud title recommendations mapped to PYP units of inquiry, useful as a starting list rather than building one from scratch (PYP Teaching Tools [9]).

## 4. Global/translated literature for international-mindedness

(Carried over from the original gap-analysis source, confirmed relevant): incorporating global literature — such as Nathan Kumar Scott's *Mangoes and Bananas*, Mahasweta Devi's *The Why-Why Girl*, and Zai Whitaker's *Kali and the Rat Snake* — helps model IB Learner Profile attributes (open-minded, principled, reflective) through story rather than direct instruction.

## Bottom line

Three deliverables would close this gap: (1) a short internal one-pager translating Scarborough's Rope into a collection-development checklist (Reading.com [10]; Just Right Reader [7]), (2) cross-referencing read-aloud picks against the current PoI themes using the Bhojwani/PYP Teaching Tools approach (Bhojwani, "PYP Librarians" [3]; PYP Teaching Tools [9]), and (3) keeping the IB assessor question list on hand ahead of the next accreditation visit, since it doubles as a self-audit checklist (Bhojwani, "IB Assessor Questions" [2]).
