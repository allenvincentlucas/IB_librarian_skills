# Gap 6: STEM, Makerspace & Advanced Research Tools

*Research notes for the Gap-Fill Addendum (Track 15) — added to the vault July 2026. In-text citations follow MLA 9th edition, with a bracketed number [n] alongside each for quick lookup against the numbered Works Cited list at the end.*

## 1. AI research assistants: what they actually do differently from ChatGPT

The key distinction worth teaching students: Elicit searches a database of roughly 138 million real academic papers and links every answer back to its source paper with supporting quotes, whereas ChatGPT generates from training data and can produce false citations — Elicit is built specifically for research, not general-purpose conversation (Fahimai [4]).

Elicit's core features worth knowing before teaching it: Paper Researcher (natural-language search across the paper database), Data Extractor (pulls specific data points into exportable tables), Notebook (chat with a set of saved papers), and Research Reports (auto-generates literature-review drafts) — though it's worth flagging to students that Elicit can miss up to 15% of relevant papers, so it should be paired with a traditional database search, not used alone (Fahimai [4]).

**Scite** does something different and complementary — rather than finding papers, it shows *how* a paper has been cited elsewhere (supporting, contrasting, or merely mentioning), which is useful for teaching students to check whether a source is actually well-regarded in its field rather than just citation-count-popular (The Effortless Academic [7]).

Academic literature review of Elicit itself is worth citing if scholarly backing is wanted rather than just marketing copy (Whitfield and Hofmann [9]).

## 2. Makerspace: cost tiers (the actual budget question)

The most directly useful resource here is **The Makerspace Librarian's Sourcebook** (ed. Ellyssa Kroski, Facet Publishing, 2017) — it includes actual costed starter-kit tiers ranging from roughly $500 for a "Low Budget, Elementary School-Focused Makerspace Starter Kit" up to $50,000 for a "Dream Budget" milling/power-equipment setup (Groves [5]).

A companion book specifically for budget-constrained schools, *The Elementary School Library Makerspace: A Start-Up Guide*, covers running a makerspace on a shoestring budget while tying activities to curriculum standards across language arts, math, science, engineering, and social studies (University at Albany Libraries [8]).

Practical equipment-list guidance if/when budget allows a 3D printer specifically (1st Makerspace [1]).

## Bottom line

Given no makerspace exists yet, the actionable next step is a $500-tier starter kit rather than jumping to 3D printing — the Kroski sourcebook's low-budget list is a ready-made shopping list (Groves [5]). For research tools, teaching Elicit and Scite together (one finds papers, one checks their credibility) is a stronger pairing than either alone (Fahimai [4]; The Effortless Academic [7]).
