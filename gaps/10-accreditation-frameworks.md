# Gap 10: Accreditation Frameworks (CIS, NEASC, WASC)

*New research added for the IB Librarian Skills website, July 2026. In-text citations follow MLA 9th edition, with a bracketed number [n] alongside each for quick lookup against the numbered Works Cited list at the end.*

## 1. Why these names keep coming up alongside the IB

The IB authorization process is functionally equivalent to accreditation for PYP, MYP, DP, and CP specifically, but it doesn't evaluate the whole school the way a general accreditor does — that's the gap CIS, NEASC, and WASC fill (SchoolVita [4]). Many international schools hold both: IB authorization for programme delivery, plus a whole-school accreditation from one or more of these regional bodies (Global Investments [8]).

**Council of International Schools (CIS)** partners directly with the U.S. regional accreditors — NEASC, WASC, and MSA — using a jointly developed protocol so a school can pursue CIS and regional U.S. accreditation in a single combined visit rather than two separate processes (Council of International Schools [1]). **NEASC**, through its Commission on International Education, accredits schools worldwide from early childhood through secondary, and is especially common among American-curriculum international schools (NEASC, "Commission on International Education" [5]). **WASC** (administered through ACSWASC) has a strong international portfolio concentrated in Asia and the Middle East, including many well-known IB schools in the Gulf (SchoolVita [4]).

## 2. What the standards actually say about libraries

This is worth knowing before assuming accreditation documentation will do the work for you: a review of multiple accreditors' published standards found library-specific language is often thin and generic — phrasing like "library resources and services are adequate for students and faculty to meet program requirements" rather than concrete expectations (Schmiedel [2]). In practice, this means the librarian usually has to build the evidence case for their own role rather than pointing to a pre-written standard, which is exactly why the *Ideal Libraries* document referenced elsewhere in this plan functions as an advocacy tool rather than a compliance checklist.

## 3. How the visit cycle actually works

NEASC's Foundation Standards are the baseline, compliance-oriented requirements — governance, curriculum, staffing qualifications — that every accredited school must evidence from day one, reviewed in depth roughly every four to five years alongside required Annual Updates in between (NEASC, "For International Schools" [6]). A school moving between accreditors (for example switching to or adding NEASC) goes through a two-day Foundation Standards Visit, and NEASC will generally honor the school's existing accreditation cycle from CIS, WASC, MSA, Cognia, or COBIS rather than starting the clock over (NEASC, "Accreditation Pathways" [3]).

## Primary sources worth bookmarking

- CIS FAQs, explaining the joint accreditation model directly (Council of International Schools [1])
- NEASC's own Standards page, listing the current Foundation Standards and Elements framework (NEASC, "Standards" [7])

## Bottom line

Three deliverables would close this gap: (1) confirming which accreditor(s) your specific school currently holds and where you are in that four-to-five-year visit cycle, since library evidence-gathering should ramp up roughly a year ahead of a full Visit, (2) building your own library-specific evidence file now rather than waiting for a standard to name it explicitly, given how generic the published library language tends to be (Schmiedel [2]), and (3) keeping the *Ideal Libraries* advocacy framing on hand specifically for accreditation self-study writing, since it's designed for exactly that audience.
